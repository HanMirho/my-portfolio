Some context

As I mentioned in the other case study, when I joined Digikala (biggest e-commerce in Iran and MENA, basically Amazon for Iran), one of the biggest gaps I noticed was the absence of a real guideline for content design and our voice. What existed was a loose set of instructions and traditions that were mostly ignored by product teams.

At the same time, Digikala was facing challenges in public opinion. Our NPS scores were declining, and I suspected that the way I wrote to users did not align with our values or brand identity. To confirm this, I proposed a research project in collaboration with the UX Research team.


1.Objectives

I wanted to answer three main questions:

  • What tone of voice do users currently perceive from Digikala's product?
  • Does this match our intended brand voice and values?
  • How can we use these insights as a baseline for revamping our UX writing guidelines?

My belief was that defining or rewriting guidelines would be meaningless unless we could also prove that users perceived them as intended.
I chose NNG's 4-dimensional model as our framework for measuring our ToV & its effects. I then designed a survey that would ask users to score Digikala on each axis. This would give us a quantifiable snapshot of how our voice was being received.


2.Challenges & experiment design

The main challenge here was designing the research with the right inclusion and exclusion criteria. I considered two approaches:

  1. Pair each question with excerpts of existing UX copy from the product.
    • Pros: Focuses users on UX copy specifically and prevents them from thinking about unrelated texts from marketing, SMS, or ads.
    • Cons: Forces users to rate texts in isolation, outside the context of their real relationship with the brand.
  2. Ask users to score us based on their overall experience with Digikala.
    • Pros: Keeps the context intact and reflects how users actually perceive the brand, in addition to excluding the UGC on the platform.
    • Cons: Includes influence from non-UX writing channels, making it harder to isolate UX copy's impact

I chose to go with both approaches, starting with an overall assessment, because:

  • It kept the user in the context of their full brand experience.
  • It would target UX copy and give us an assessment of its situation, along with writing from other departments.
  • Because of the previous point, the results would be useful beyond the product team, benefiting marketing, CX, and support as well.

3.Aaaaand we got laid-off.

Unfortunately, the project never made it past the planning stage. Digikala entered a merger process, and soon after, both the UX Research and UX Writing teams were hit by layoffs.

Still, I believe the project was novel and promising. Using the survey to score our tone, we could have set a strong, data-backed, foundation for fresh and enforceable guidelines. It also could have fostered more cooperation across departments, which Digikala sorely lacked.
