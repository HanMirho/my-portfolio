Studying a Failure

How I tried to set up a solid UXW framework at a large enterprise.

Tl;dr

-- Was brought on to Digikala.com as a solo Senior UX Writer.

-- Was tasked to build up a comprehensive content design strategy, codify the neglected tone and voice, and integrate UX Writing workflow into the Design workflow.

-- Planned the research, created the strategy, made all the diagrams, flowcharts, and presentations that corporate looooves. Did a bunch of negotiating, horse-trading, and compromising to get stakeholder buy-in.

-- Company went through a merger, directions changed and layoffs ensued before any comprehensive change. :(


1. Getting a feel for the status quo

The onboarding process was quick and snappy. That was mainly because there wasn't much to be onboarded on.
Digikala, with more than a 1000 tech and product employees, had:

  • No unifying framework or workflow for UX Writing.
  • No codified tone of voice guidelines (and the little that existed was ignored by the product department).
  • No support for the role of UX writers: writers were treated as “copy polishers” rather than partners in shaping IA, or mental models.

But these were the challenges I was brought on to handle. So I got on to it.


2. What I did

I proposed and carried out how UX Writing fit into product development:

  • New workflow & structure: UX Writers would join projects at the same stage as product designers, ensuring content shaped the user experience from the start.
  • Horse-trading: Negotiated with PMs, design leads, and junior execs to secure enough buy-in to pitch directly to the Chief Product Officer.
  • AI for content inventory: We had no source of truth, designs almost never matched production, and there was a heap of UGC to sort through. With the tech team busy, we wrote a crawler using ChatGPT that would give me a database of all content we had written, along with their urls.
  • Team structure: Advocated for increasing the 'team' headcount from 1 to 3 UX Writers, so that every two squads shared at least one dedicated writer.
  • Discover ToV perception using UXR: Launched a joint research project to serve as the foundations of a research-validated tone of voice guideline, making content more consistent and user-centered. (This has its own case study!)
  • Social listening for mental models: We also needed to see what verbs and adjectives people were using to refer to describe the action of using a number of our new and old features. With the help of a kindred soul in the data team, we started a side-project to set up a listener for certain features and words on social media.

3. How it fell apart

Right when momentum was building, the business strategy shifted dramatically:

  • Digikala was secretly preparing to sell to the Hamrah-e-Avval holding group.
  • Leadership changes and cost-cutting measures deprioritized UX and content.
  • The changing of the guard dampened momentum, and my proposals never reached full implementation.
  • Soon after, layoffs hit—affecting UX Writers, designers, researchers, and PMs. I was among those let go.


4.Little wins; and other take aways

This case study isn't a march of triumph, as most others are. But there are people and projects that I still remember fondly. I also learned a couple of lessons:

  • Diagnoses can be easy. But implementing change depends on timing, politics, leadership buy-in, and sometimes, even the damned weather.

  • UX Writing needs structural support. Climbing the UX maturity pyramid without organizational support for proper headcount, workflow, and cultural understanding, is almost impossible. Without it, the role will be marginalized.

  • Change is brittle. Business priorities can shift overnight, and even well-designed strategies can collapse if they don't change with the C-suite's agenda.
